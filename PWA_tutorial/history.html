Origins of PWAs

While Google coined the term Progressive Web Apps (PWAs) in 2015, the core idea traces back to 2007 when Steve Jobs introduced web apps for the iPhone‚Äîapps that looked and behaved like native ones, powered by Safari. However, Apple soon pivoted to native app development, launching the App Store later that year.

Google's Reinvention

In 2015, Alex Russel and Frances Berriman reintroduced the concept as PWAs‚Äîweb experiences combining native app performance with web accessibility. Google and Microsoft heavily promoted PWAs as a bridge between native and web apps.

Why PWAs Became Important

Before PWAs, users had to choose between the reach of websites and the functionality of native apps. PWAs combine the best of both worlds ‚Äî the accessibility of the web and the performance of native applications ‚Äî making them a vital step in the evolution of modern software development.

  • Web apps: accessible everywhere, but limited in offline capability and native integration.
  • Native apps: powerful and integrated, but expensive to build and deploy on multiple platforms.

PWAs bridge this gap by delivering:

  • Fast loading and offline access via caching.
  • Installability without app stores.
  • Unified experience across devices.
  • Lower development and maintenance costs.

Industry Adoption Examples

  • Twitter Lite: reported 65% increase in pages per session after adopting PWA architecture.
  • Starbucks: their PWA is 99% smaller than the iOS app, dramatically improving reach.
  • Pinterest: saw a 40% increase in time spent after launching their PWA.
  • Trivago: offline bookmarking and improved engagement metrics after PWA rollout.

These success stories illustrate the performance and engagement benefits of PWAs across industries.
